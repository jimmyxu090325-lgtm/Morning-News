1. US TECH AND AI

The dominant narrative in US tech this week sits at the intersection of three converging forces: Nvidia's continued AI infrastructure supremacy, Apple's 50th anniversary pivot toward artificial intelligence, and an increasingly complex geopolitical backdrop that is reshaping supply chains and investor risk appetite in ways that are difficult to fully price.

On Nvidia, the market appears to be working through a genuine valuation tension. Analysts are now projecting that Nvidia's Blackwell and Rubin chip generations will generate $1 trillion in lifetime sales, a figure that is both staggering and, according to market observers, seemingly already shrugged off by equity investors. This is a meaningful signal. When a $1 trillion revenue projection fails to move a stock, it suggests either that the market has already priced in an extraordinarily optimistic long-term scenario, or that near-term macro and geopolitical headwinds are suppressing the multiple. Likely both. The Coupang partnership announced at the NVIDIA AI Conference, combining DGX SuperPOD infrastructure with Coupang's logistics cloud, is emblematic of how Nvidia is embedding itself into vertical industry workflows beyond the hyperscaler customer base. Logistics AI is a high-frequency, latency-sensitive domain, and winning design-ins here diversifies Nvidia's revenue stream while validating Blackwell's commercial applicability outside pure model training.

The humanoid robotics story is quietly gathering complexity. A Reuters-sourced report confirms that Tesla and other US robotics firms are sourcing critical components from Chinese suppliers, a dependency that sits uncomfortably at the intersection of Washington's export control ambitions and Beijing's own strategic industrial policy. This is not merely a supply chain footnote. If Washington moves to restrict component sourcing from China in the robotics sector, the cost structures of US humanoid robot manufacturers could deteriorate sharply, and the timeline for commercial viability could be pushed back considerably. Watch for any USTR or Commerce Department commentary on this in coming weeks.

Apple's 50th anniversary has coincided with a notable strategic repositioning that deserves serious analytical attention. The company is opening Siri to third-party AI assistants on iOS, a move that signals a pragmatic acknowledgment that Apple cannot win the AI race on its own. Wedbush's Dan Ives frames this correctly: the next decade for Apple is an AI story, not a hardware story. With a $3.6 trillion market cap and Tim Cook's succession increasingly a boardroom topic, the company faces a dual test of sustaining services revenue growth while credibly entering the AI monetization race. Warren Buffett's comment that he sold Apple too soon and would buy more under different market conditions is a meaningful endorsement of the long-term thesis, even as he signals caution about current conditions. Meanwhile, Amazon's reported $9 billion pursuit of Globalstar has direct read-throughs for Apple, which already uses Globalstar's satellite infrastructure for emergency SOS features on iPhones. A change in ownership of that infrastructure introduces both risk and potential strategic optionality for Apple in satellite-based connectivity.

Microsoft's $10 billion Japan AI infrastructure commitment, executed in partnership with SoftBank, is the latest evidence that the hyperscaler capex cycle remains firmly intact despite equity market volatility. Japan's Sakura Internet surged 20% on the news, illustrating how AI infrastructure spending is creating investable secondary beneficiaries across Asian markets. The broader AI infrastructure theme continues to reward picks-and-shovels exposure, though Blue Owl's decision to cap redemptions at 5% on its private credit funds, citing heightened concerns about AI-related disruption to software companies, is a sober reminder that the AI trade has a deflationary underside for incumbent software businesses that have not yet demonstrated credible AI integration.

Heading into earnings season, caution is warranted on names that carry high expectations without near-term earnings catalysts. The Nebius growth story is attracting speculative interest, but the risk-reward calculus at current valuations for high-growth AI infrastructure plays requires a long time horizon and tolerance for significant drawdowns.

2. GLOBAL MACRO

The macro environment as of April 3rd is best characterized as a fragile, oil-shock-driven stagflation risk overlay on an economy that was already decelerating. The central geopolitical catalyst remains the Iran war, which began in late February and has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz to normal transit, triggering cascading effects across energy, shipping, inflation, and risk sentiment that are still working their way through the system.

The March jobs report, due for release today, is the most consequential near-term data point. Consensus expectations are for 59,000 nonfarm payroll additions and an unchanged unemployment rate of 4.4%. That payroll figure, if confirmed, would represent a sharp deceleration from recent trend and would place the labor market unambiguously in softening territory. The unemployment rate at 4.4% is already elevated relative to the post-pandemic lows, and a weak payroll print today will intensify the debate about whether the Fed faces a stagflationary bind or a straightforward softening that eventually justifies rate cuts. The market has apparently priced in rates holding for the rest of the year, a view UBS has flagged as having direct negative implications for gold if sustained, since bullion's rally partly reflects rate cut expectations. Gold's bull run faces a technical hurdle here, though the war risk premium and reserve diversification demand from central banks provide a structural floor that prevents a clean reversal.

Oil remains the most important macro variable. Jet fuel costs have surged more than 80% since the Iran war began, a figure that is already feeding through into airline economics, with United Airlines the second US carrier in a week to raise checked baggage fees as carriers attempt to pass costs on to consumers. Mortgage rates have now risen for five consecutive weeks and are at multi-year highs, directly suppressing housing affordability and acting as a brake on consumer spending. The feedback loop from oil to fuel to transportation costs to consumer inflation is real and accelerating, and it is placing the Fed in a genuinely uncomfortable position. Any further escalation in the Hormuz situation would exacerbate this dynamic meaningfully.

There is, however, a tentative positive signal worth monitoring. Asia-Pacific markets opened mostly higher overnight on hopes that the Hormuz Strait may be moving toward a reopening framework. Iran and Oman are reportedly drafting a protocol to monitor Hormuz traffic, which, if formalized, could take meaningful war risk premium out of crude prices relatively quickly. The read-through to equities would be broadly positive, particularly for consumer discretionary, airlines, and cruise lines, which have been among the hardest hit by the oil shock and associated demand concerns.

The DXY and Treasury yields are caught in a cross-current. A weak jobs report today would logically push the 10-year yield lower and weaken the dollar, but if oil prices remain elevated and inflation expectations stay sticky, the long end of the curve faces upward pressure from term premium, complicating the duration trade. The net macro positioning is risk-cautious, with investors in the US and Europe generally reducing exposure to high-beta names ahead of a data-heavy and geopolitically uncertain period. Saudi business districts shifting to remote work protocols following Iranian threats against US firms is a data point that suggests the conflict's geographic and commercial reach is widening rather than contracting.

Warren Buffett's public comments this week are themselves a macro signal. His warnings about nuclear escalation risk, his acknowledgment of selling Apple too soon, and his description of a new buy as tiny all paint a picture of a 95-year-old capital allocator who remains engaged but is deliberately holding powder dry in an environment he views as structurally riskier than at any prior point in his investing career. When Buffett publicly invokes nuclear risk, sophisticated investors should treat that as a serious tail risk flag.

3. A-SHARE AND CHINA MARKETS

Chinese equity markets are navigating a distinct set of dynamics that partially decouple them from the US risk-off sentiment, though the oil shock and global demand slowdown create unavoidable linkages. The most notable behavioral signal is that mainland Chinese investors extended their buying of Hong Kong stocks for the third consecutive month in March, purchasing approximately HK$6 billion in net inflows despite the Iran war turbulence. This sustained southbound flow suggests domestic institutional and retail investors view Chinese and Hong Kong assets as relatively insulated from, or even benefiting from, the dislocation in Western markets caused by the energy shock.

The EV sector delivered an important positive data point, with Chinese manufacturers posting a strong sales rebound in March after a weak start to 2026. Local government subsidies and aggressive financing programs drove first-time buyer conversions, and momentum is expected to extend into the second quarter. Leapmotor's strategic moves are illustrative of the broader ambition: the company is establishing a European R&D hub and exploring Canadian assembly, signaling that Chinese EV makers are serious about building durable global market positions rather than simply exporting finished goods. This structural shift toward localized manufacturing and R&D presence in target markets is a response to both tariff risks and political pressure in Western markets, and it represents a more sophisticated and resilient globalization strategy than the pure export model of the prior decade.

Three niche commodity prices are reportedly surging, driven by China's supply chain dominance in materials critical to defense applications and AI semiconductor production. This is a theme with direct relevance to several of the A-share focus stocks and deserves careful monitoring. If the Iran war continues to create demand spikes for defense-related materials and AI chip production ramps require sustained commodity inputs, companies positioned in the upstream supply of these materials could see meaningful earnings upgrades through the balance of the year.

On the technology side, TCL CSOT's unveiling of Super Pixel display technology ahead of MWC 2026 reflects the continued competitiveness of Chinese display manufacturers. TCL's position in Mini LED and ultra-large format displays remains strong globally, and the Lenovo collaboration on rollable and foldable device display architectures is worth monitoring as a potential catalyst for the broader display materials supply chain.

CAS Space's filing for a Star Market listing is a signal that Beijing's support for commercial space is generating a capital markets pipeline of investable names. This is part of a broader pattern in which strategic sectors identified in Beijing's industrial policy roadmap, including space, AI, humanoid robotics, and advanced materials, are being supported both through direct government programs and through facilitated capital market access. The policy tailwind for these sectors on A-shares remains intact and is, if anything, being reinforced by the geopolitical environment, which strengthens the domestic case for technology self-sufficiency.

Huawei's cloud revenue decline in 2025 when excluding internal customers is a reminder that the buildout of Chinese AI infrastructure, while accelerating, faces genuine commercial adoption challenges. The competitive gap with US hyperscalers in pure AI model performance remains a headwind for Chinese cloud providers selling to external enterprise customers, and this creates an uneven landscape within the A-share tech sector between companies benefiting from government-backed AI rollout and those dependent on organic commercial demand.

Hong Kong's role as a launchpad for China Inc.'s global expansion continues to evolve, with the SC conference this week reinforcing its function as the strategic staging ground for Chinese enterprises transitioning from export models to fully integrated global business ecosystems. New World Development's decision to shelve the Causeway Bay acquisition reflects persistent caution among Hong Kong developers despite improving core office demand, suggesting the property recovery is gradual and uneven rather than broad-based.

4. STOCK SPOTLIGHT

World Century Huatong / Century Huatong (002602):
Century Huatong operates across gaming and automotive electronics, two sectors with divergent near-term trajectories. The gaming segment faces ongoing regulatory and monetization pressures in China's domestic market, while the automotive electronics and connected vehicle businesses are positioned to benefit from the broader EV supply chain expansion. Watch for any announcements tied to partnerships with domestic EV manufacturers or AI-driven in-vehicle system integrations. Given the strong EV sales rebound in March, any positive read-through to automotive electronics suppliers could act as a near-term catalyst. The stock has likely experienced range-bound trading in recent weeks and requires a specific product or partnership catalyst to break higher.

CITIC Metal / CITIC Metal (601061):
This is one of the most directly relevant names in the current macro environment. With niche critical commodity prices surging in the wake of the Iran war, and with CITIC Metal's exposure to metals essential for defense and semiconductor supply chains, the fundamental backdrop is improving. Mainland buying of Hong Kong and A-share assets creates additional support. Watch for any guidance revisions or commodity price-linked earnings upgrades in the near term. The strategic narrative around China's supply chain grip on critical materials is a multi-quarter tailwind for this name.

Minmetals New Energy / Minmetals New Energy (002628):
Positioned at the intersection of the energy transition and China's critical materials supply chain, Minmetals New Energy benefits from both the domestic EV rebound and the broader commodity price surge in materials relevant to battery and energy storage production. The strong March EV sales data is a direct positive read-through for battery materials demand. Monitor any policy announcements from NDRC or MIIT regarding new energy vehicle or energy storage subsidy extensions, as these would directly support forward volume assumptions for the company's key customers.

Hengbang Co. / Hengbang Co. (002237):
Hengbang operates in precious and non-ferrous metal smelting, making it a beneficiary of the current commodities surge. Gold's continued bull run, even if facing near-term rate-driven headwinds, and elevated base metal prices driven by supply chain disruptions related to the Iran conflict create a constructive revenue environment. The key risk is margin compression if input costs rise faster than realized metal prices. Watch refining spread data and any commodity price consolidation signals, as a Hormuz reopening could partially relieve some of the war risk premium embedded in current metal prices.

Yake Technology / Yake Technology (002409):
Yake Technology's specialty chemical and semiconductor materials business makes it directly relevant to the AI infrastructure build-out theme and the surging demand for semiconductor inputs. The news that niche commodity prices tied to AI chip production are rising is a specific tailwind. Additionally, any tightening of export controls by either the US or China affecting semiconductor materials would likely benefit domestic Chinese suppliers like Yake. The geopolitical backdrop is fundamentally constructive for this name over the medium term. Watch for any specific MIIT or MOFCOM policy announcements on semiconductor material self-sufficiency.

Three Gorges Energy / Three Gorges Energy (600905):
As one of China's largest renewable energy operators, Three Gorges Energy sits within a policy-favored sector. The elevated oil price environment caused by the Hormuz closure paradoxically strengthens the long-term investment case for renewables by highlighting the strategic vulnerability of fossil fuel dependence. Near-term, watch for any power price mechanism updates from the NDRC and the trajectory of wind and solar installation targets in the upcoming policy cycle. Utilities of this profile tend to be relative defensives in a risk-off domestic environment, which is worth noting given current market uncertainty.

PowerChina / PowerChina (601669):
PowerChina is a major engineering, procurement, and construction player in the energy infrastructure space, with significant exposure to both domestic grid and renewable energy projects as well as overseas Belt and Road-linked contracts. The elevated oil price environment creates mixed signals: higher energy investment budgets from oil-producing Belt and Road partners benefit the overseas order book, but rising input costs in construction create margin pressure. Domestically, continued government spending on grid modernization and renewable capacity additions supports the domestic project pipeline. Watch for new contract announcements and any updates to the overseas project backlog, particularly in the Middle East and Central Asia where the Iran conflict may be redirecting energy infrastructure investment flows.

5. KEY THEMES TO WATCH TODAY

March US Jobs Report Release: The 59,000 consensus payroll estimate is a critical inflection point. A miss to the downside would accelerate recession concerns and complicate the Fed's already difficult balance between inflation control and growth support. A surprise to the upside would provide temporary relief but may also reinforce the higher-for-longer rate narrative that is suppressing equity multiples. Either way, expect elevated volatility in Treasuries and the dollar immediately following the release, with cascading effects across risk assets including A-shares through the offshore sentiment channel.

Hormuz Strait Diplomatic Developments: The Iran-Oman protocol drafting process is the single most important geopolitical variable in today's session. Any credible signal of a phased reopening of Hormuz would trigger a meaningful relief rally in airlines, cruise lines, consumer discretionary, and energy-importing economies, while putting downward pressure on crude and relieving some inflation anxiety. Conversely, any new military incident or escalatory statement from either side would reset risk sentiment sharply lower and push oil higher again.

Nvidia and AI Infrastructure Sentiment: With the Blackwell $1 trillion sales projection failing to generate a positive market reaction, watch whether today brings any additional analyst commentary or institutional repositioning. The market's apparent indifference to extraordinary forward revenue numbers from Nvidia suggests a valuation debate is quietly intensifying, and any negative datapoint, whether from supply chain, export controls, or hyperscaler capex guidance, could trigger a technically-driven correction in AI names.

China Critical Commodities and Supply Chain Policy: The surge in niche commodity prices tied to defense and AI semiconductor production, occurring against a backdrop of intensified geopolitical competition, is creating a policy activation risk for both Washington and Beijing. Any announcement of new Chinese export restrictions on critical materials or US countermeasures affecting Chinese material imports would have immediate and significant price implications for the relevant A-share names in this briefing, particularly CITIC Metal, Minmetals New Energy, and Yake Technology.

Apple AI Strategy and Succession Narrative: As Apple's 50th anniversary coverage continues, watch for any additional detail on the Siri third-party AI integration roadmap or any succession-related commentary from the board or major institutional holders. The Amazon-Globalstar deal, if confirmed, introduces a meaningful new variable in Apple's satellite services dependency. Any move by Apple to respond strategically, whether through a competing acquisition, a deeper partnership, or an in-house satellite program announcement, would represent a material near-term catalyst for AAPL and its ecosystem suppliers.
