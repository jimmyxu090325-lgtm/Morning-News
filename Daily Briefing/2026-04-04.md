1. US TECH & AI

The AI supercycle narrative remains intact but is entering a more competitive and geopolitically complicated phase. The dominant theme this week is not whether AI infrastructure spending continues — it clearly does — but rather who captures the most value from it and under what constraints. The AMD-versus-Nvidia debate is crystallizing: AMD has deepened its Asian AI footprint by announcing a sovereign AI infrastructure partnership with NAVER Cloud in South Korea and expanding its HBM4 supply collaboration with Samsung, a dual move that simultaneously addresses the memory bottleneck problem and establishes a strategic beachhead in a region where US-China tensions are redirecting chip procurement. Nvidia retains the architectural and software ecosystem lead, but AMD is no longer a token challenger. The AI supercycle, as one analyst framed it this week, is large enough to sustain both meaningfully.

The High-Bandwidth Memory bottleneck deserves particular attention. Google's TurboQuant breakthrough has partially relieved quantization-related memory pressure, but analysts are correctly noting that HBM demand structurally exceeds supply at the frontier model tier. This dynamic is a genuine catalyst for Micron, which has exposure to HBM3e and is positioning for HBM4. Billionaire Steve Cohen's Point72 has been adding both Arista Networks and Taiwan Semiconductor to its 13F portfolio, which is a signal worth noting: Cohen's positioning suggests continued confidence in the AI networking layer and the foundry backbone, not just the chip designers themselves. TSM in particular faces a delicate moment given the Iran war's effect on critical mineral supply chains, which we address in the macro section, but its secular position as the world's indispensable fab is undiminished.

On Apple, Warren Buffett delivered a characteristically candid assessment at what appears to be a Berkshire shareholder-adjacent event: he acknowledged selling Apple too soon and said he would buy more, though not in this market environment. That qualifier is important. Buffett's reluctance to add at current valuations, combined with his general wariness about elevated market pricing, reinforces the cautionary tone that runs through several pieces in this morning's feed. Apple's stock is Berkshire's largest holding even after last year's trimming, and the company itself made news this week by showcasing 50 years of unreleased prototype history — a reminder of its brand depth — while also navigating the one-year anniversary of Trump's Liberation Day tariffs, which continue to create cost and supply chain friction for a company so deeply tied to Asian manufacturing.

OpenAI's internal dynamics add a layer of uncertainty to the AI narrative. The medical leave of Fidji Simo, who had been overseeing product, is being covered by president Greg Brockman in an interim capacity. Meanwhile, OpenAI's acquisition of media company TBPN — the tech podcast that cultivated a Silicon Valley following before going mainstream — is drawing skeptical commentary, with critics calling the M&A strategy "vibe-driven" rather than strategically coherent. The acquisition follows the eye-catching $6.4 billion commitment to Jony Ive's hardware startup. The pattern raises legitimate questions about capital allocation discipline at a company still burning cash. None of this changes OpenAI's central role in the AI ecosystem, but it introduces execution risk at the leadership and product layer.

Amazon's reported talks to acquire Globalstar as part of its Project Kuiper satellite expansion are strategically logical: SpaceX's Starlink has demonstrated that low-earth orbit connectivity is both technically viable and commercially valuable, and Amazon needs a spectrum and infrastructure shortcut. This comes in the same week that SpaceX floated an IPO valuation above $2 trillion — up from $1.8 trillion at earlier estimates — with a possible June listing that could raise up to $75 billion. That valuation, if achieved, would place SpaceX above most of the S&P 500 by market cap and would represent a landmark moment for private space infrastructure investment. The broader space sector had a strong week, with Planet Labs, Iridium, Intuitive Machines, and Redwire all rising, the latter benefiting from its confirmed role as a key partner in NASA's Artemis II mission.

Amplify's QDVO fund, which converts Magnificent Seven equity exposure into a covered-call income vehicle yielding 10.7% monthly, highlights a real and growing investor dilemma: in a volatile, tariff-stressed, geopolitically elevated market, income harvesting via options strategies on mega-cap tech is attracting capital that might otherwise have stayed long equity. The flow dynamic is worth monitoring as a sentiment indicator.

2. GLOBAL MACRO
----------------------------------------------------------------------

The macro environment this morning is being pulled in sharply opposing directions, and the tension between them is the defining challenge for portfolio positioning. On one side, this morning's nonfarm payroll print was significantly stronger than expected: 178,000 jobs were added in March against a consensus forecast of just 59,000, while the unemployment rate ticked up to 4.3% from 4.4%. The headline beats consensus by a factor of three, which under normal circumstances would read as unambiguously bullish for risk assets and would reprice Fed rate cut expectations toward fewer and later moves. But these are not normal circumstances.

The Iran war is now an active and escalating variable. A US fighter jet was shot down over or near Iranian territory, with one crew member rescued. President Trump has threatened to destroy Iranian infrastructure and stated that the US military will strike Tehran, quote, extremely hard for the next two to three weeks. This is not background noise. The combination of active military conflict with a major oil-producing nation and a credible escalation threat creates immediate upward pressure on crude oil prices and introduces a tail risk premium across all asset classes. Warren Buffett's publicly stated concern this week — that an Iranian nuclear capability would make a global nuclear disaster harder to avoid — reflects a risk assessment that sophisticated investors cannot dismiss as hyperbolic. Berkshire's posture, as Buffett indicated, remains cautious on adding risk in this environment.

The commodity market is already registering the stress. Three niche critical element prices are surging in the wake of the Iran war, specifically materials used in defense applications and AI semiconductor manufacturing. This is a China supply chain story as much as it is a war story: China's dominant position in rare earth processing and critical mineral refining means that any disruption to global trade flows, whether from tariffs or conflict-related logistics disruption, feeds directly into input cost inflation for semiconductor and defense manufacturers. Copper, a proxy for global industrial activity, is caught between the positive signal from the payroll beat and the negative signal from geopolitical risk. Gold's direction this morning should be watched carefully as a real-time read on whether the safe-haven bid is dominating.

The one-year anniversary of Liberation Day tariffs this week has prompted a wave of retrospective analysis. The conclusion is nuanced: some sectors — particularly retail and autos — have absorbed meaningful and lasting structural damage to their cost models, while others have adapted through supply chain diversification. The net effect on inflation has been sticky rather than transitory, which is precisely what makes the Fed's positioning so difficult. A strong jobs number under normal conditions would push Treasury yields higher and delay cuts. In today's context, that same strong number coexists with a geopolitical shock that could simultaneously push energy prices higher and compress growth expectations. The result is likely a steepening of the yield curve with elevated volatility rather than a clean directional move. The MarketWatch feed flags that the Treasury bond market is showing increasing worry about inflation among retirement-age investors, which is consistent with long-end yields remaining under upward pressure.

The DXY dollar index faces cross-cutting forces: safe-haven demand from the Iran conflict argues for dollar strength, while the accumulating weight of fiscal expansion, tariff-driven trade partner retaliation, and questions about US institutional credibility argue for structural dollar softness over a medium-term horizon. Today's session will likely see the dollar bid initially on the payroll strength and safe-haven demand, with the more nuanced view playing out over the coming weeks.

Blue Owl's decision to cap redemptions in its private credit funds at 5% after unusually high withdrawal requests is a notable stress signal. The firm attributed the surge to investor concerns about AI-related disruption to software companies — a reminder that the private credit market's exposure to venture-backed and growth software firms creates a transmission mechanism between AI competitive disruption and credit market stability that is not fully priced by public market participants.

3. A-SHARE / CHINA MARKETS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

China's market narrative this morning is constructively complex. On the positive side, there are genuine green shoots in corporate strategy and technology positioning. On the cautionary side, valuations, earnings quality concerns, and the geopolitical overhang from the Iran war — which affects China's energy import costs and its critical mineral export leverage — create a mixed picture for near-term direction.

Tencent's expansion of its OpenClaw enterprise AI agent suite is the headline A-share adjacent story of the morning. The "lobster" AI agent frenzy in China — a colloquial reference to the wave of enthusiasm around autonomous AI agents capable of executing multi-step tasks — has become a genuine product cycle driver for Chinese internet and cloud companies. Tencent's ClawPro launch targets enterprise deployment, which is the commercially durable tier of this cycle versus the consumer hype layer. This puts Tencent in direct competition with Alibaba's Qwen-based enterprise tools and Baidu's AI cloud stack. The competitive intensity is high, but the market opportunity is large enough that multiple platforms can scale simultaneously, at least in the near term. Huawei's reported decline in cloud computing revenue in 2025 — even as the company claims growth when internal customers are included — suggests that the domestic cloud AI race is more competitive and less certain than the nationalistic framing sometimes implies.

Hong Kong is actively positioning itself as the internationalization launchpad for China Inc., with the 2026 SCMP conference bringing together Chinese enterprises pivoting from traditional export models to building global ecosystems. This is a structural theme with multi-year implications: companies like Leapmotor, which is opening a European R&D center and exploring Canadian assembly, represent a new generation of Chinese industrial champions that are building genuine overseas operational capacity rather than just export relationships. The RMB internationalization angle and the Belt and Road infrastructure thread both feed into this story, and Chinese financial institutions are facilitating it aggressively.

Tesla's recapture of the global pure EV sales crown from BYD in Q1 2026 is a nuanced data point for A-share investors. BYD remains dominant in China's domestic market and is growing its hybrid business rapidly, and Tesla's margin structure continues to face pressure. But the headline is psychologically significant and may temper some of the more extreme bullish sentiment around domestic EV makers' global competitiveness narratives. The broader EV supply chain — batteries, cathode materials, power electronics — remains a CSI 300 sector weight that institutional investors cannot ignore.

Pop Mart's inability to arrest its share price decline despite six consecutive buybacks in recent days is a warning signal for A-share growth consumer names. The company's dependence on the Labubu IP is becoming a liability narrative: when a single character or franchise accounts for a disproportionate share of revenue and margin, the market appropriately assigns a concentration discount. This is a sector-specific story, but it echoes a broader theme: Chinese consumer discretionary names that rode a single product cycle are facing valuation compression as growth normalization becomes apparent.

New World Development's decision to shelve its Causeway Bay acquisition speaks to continued developer caution in Hong Kong commercial real estate despite some demand recovery signals at the margin. For A-share property-adjacent names, this reinforces the view that the sector's recovery is uneven and that capital discipline is now structurally embedded in developer decision-making in a way it was not in 2019 or 2020. NDRC and PBOC policy signals remain supportive at the macro level, but the transmission to asset prices in property and construction is slow and uneven.

The MSCI February 2026 index review changes — though announced earlier — continue to affect institutional flows into and out of specific A-share names. TCL Technology and its CSOT display subsidiary continue to generate positive product cycle news from MWC and CES, with rollable display and Super Pixel technology generating genuine commercial interest that should support the stock's valuation premium within the display hardware sector.

4. STOCK SPOTLIGHT
----------------------------------------------------------------------

世纪华通 / Century Huatong (002602):
Century Huatong continues to trade at the intersection of two major A-share themes: mobile gaming and AI-driven content generation. The company's Shengqu Games subsidiary remains the core earnings driver, though user growth in mobile gaming has moderated. The more interesting near-term catalyst is the company's positioning in AI-generated game content and virtual world infrastructure, which aligns with the broader lobster AI agent frenzy in China's tech sector. Watch for any announcements tying its game IP to AI agent platforms or large model partnerships. Valuation remains elevated relative to earnings growth, so execution on AI monetization is the key variable. The geopolitical environment and tariff overhang do not directly affect this business, but broad risk-off sentiment from Iran escalation could pressure growth multiples across the board today.

中信金属 / CITIC Metal (601061):
This is one of the most directly relevant names to watch today given the macro backdrop. The CNBC Economy feed explicitly flags surging prices for critical elements used in defense and AI semiconductors in the wake of the Iran war, and China's grip on supply chains for these materials is the central analytical point. CITIC Metal, as a state-linked metals trading and investment platform with exposure to copper, cobalt, and strategic minerals, sits squarely in this thesis. If critical element prices are indeed surging, CITIC Metal's underlying asset values and trading margins improve in a correlated manner. The stock may see renewed institutional interest as a geopolitical hedge and a critical minerals proxy. Watch copper spot prices and any policy signals from MOFCOM or NDRC regarding export controls on strategic materials, which China could deploy as a trade war countermeasure.

五矿新能 / Minmetals New Energy (002628):
Minmetals New Energy operates at the intersection of state-owned resources capital and the energy transition supply chain, with exposure to lithium, cobalt, and new energy materials. Like CITIC Metal, it is a beneficiary of the critical minerals supply chain story that is intensifying under both the Iran war commodity shock and the ongoing US-China technology competition. The company's state-owned enterprise lineage gives it privileged access to overseas mining assets and domestic policy support. However, the stock has seen significant volatility this year as lithium prices have struggled to find a floor after the 2023-2024 oversupply correction. Today's catalysts are commodity price movements and any indications that the lithium destocking cycle is finally turning. The EV sector production data — including Tesla's Q1 rebound — provides incremental demand support for battery materials.

恒邦股份 / Hengbang Co. (002237):
Hengbang is a gold and copper smelting operation, which makes it a direct beneficiary of the current macro environment in a straightforward way. Gold prices, which have been elevated throughout 2025 and into 2026 on safe-haven and de-dollarization demand, provide a tailwind to smelting margins when gold is a byproduct of copper processing. With the Iran conflict now in active escalation and Buffett publicly flagging nuclear risk, safe-haven demand for gold is unlikely to abate quickly. Copper's directional uncertainty — caught between strong US jobs data and geopolitical fear — makes Hengbang's copper-side margin less predictable in the near term. Net, the gold component of the business is the more reliable earnings driver at this moment. Watch for gold spot price direction in the first hour of trading as a guide to Hengbang's intraday trajectory.

雅克科技 / Yake Technology (002409):
Yake Technology is a specialty chemicals and semiconductor materials company, operating in the advanced process chemical supply chain that supports wafer fabrication. This positions it within the broader theme of domestic semiconductor supply chain localization that Beijing has been accelerating under NDRC and MIIT guidance. The critical minerals supply chain stress highlighted in today's feed — specifically materials used in semiconductor manufacturing — is directly relevant to Yake's business environment. Higher input material costs are a headwind, but tighter supply also strengthens the case for domestic chemical process material substitution, which benefits Yake's localization positioning. The company's exposure to advanced node chemistry makes it a watch item whenever TSMC, Samsung, or domestic fabs announce capacity expansion plans. Near term, watch for any trade policy developments affecting chemical precursor imports or exports that could move the stock.

三峡能源 / Three Gorges Energy (600905):
Three Gorges Energy is China's largest listed offshore wind and utility-scale renewable energy developer, backed by the Three Gorges Group. The stock operates in a sector that receives consistent policy tailwind from Beijing's carbon neutrality commitments and the 14th and 15th Five-Year Plan renewable energy capacity targets. The more immediate catalyst to watch is grid connection progress on offshore wind projects and any updates on the domestic green power certificate pricing mechanism, which affects revenue realization for wind and solar assets. The Iran war's effect on global energy prices is, paradoxically, a mild long-term positive for renewables investment rationale in China, as it reinforces energy security arguments for domestic clean energy buildout. Near term, the stock tends to trade with bond-proxy characteristics, so any yield curve moves driven by today's macro data will influence its relative attractiveness versus fixed income alternatives.

中国电建 / PowerChina (601669):
PowerChina is the dominant state-owned engineering and construction conglomerate for power infrastructure, with a particularly strong position in hydropower, nuclear, wind, and cross-border Belt and Road energy projects. Two themes from today's briefing are directly relevant. First, the energy security anxiety created by the Iran conflict strengthens the policy case for accelerating domestic power infrastructure investment, which feeds directly into PowerChina's order book. Second, the SCMP's coverage of China Inc.'s global expansion strategy, particularly in infrastructure, positions PowerChina as a core execution vehicle for overseas energy project awards in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East. The stock trades at a modest valuation relative to its backlog and state backing, but execution risk on overseas projects in geopolitically sensitive regions has historically been underappreciated by the market. Watch for any NDRC infrastructure stimulus announcements and Belt and Road project update disclosures as near-term catalysts.

5. KEY THEMES TO WATCH TODAY
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Iran escalation is the session's dominant risk variable. President Trump's stated intention to strike Tehran hard over the next two to three weeks, combined with a confirmed aircraft incident, creates an energy price shock risk and a safe-haven asset bid that could override positive economic data signals in US and Asian markets alike. Monitor crude oil spot prices and gold in real time as the primary gauges of how aggressively the market is pricing this tail risk.

The US payroll beat creates a genuine Fed policy dilemma that will drive Treasury yield volatility throughout the session. At 178,000 versus a 59,000 consensus, the March jobs number is unambiguously strong and pushes against near-term rate cut expectations. However, if the Iran conflict simultaneously pushes energy inflation higher and growth expectations lower, the Fed faces a stagflationary setup that is the worst-case scenario for duration assets. Watch the 10-year Treasury yield and the 2s10s spread as the market's real-time verdict on this tension.

Critical minerals and defense supply chain names deserve close attention as a China-specific trade. The explicit surge in niche commodity prices tied to defense and AI semiconductors, as reported in the CNBC Economy feed, directly affects A-share names with rare earth, cobalt, lithium, and strategic metals exposure. CITIC Metal, Minmetals New Energy, and the broader rare earth complex are the primary beneficiaries, and any hint of Chinese export restriction policy deployment as a trade countermeasure would amplify this move sharply.

AI infrastructure competitive dynamics are shifting at the partnership and supply chain layer in ways that matter for semiconductor positioning. AMD's dual announcement — sovereign AI with NAVER Cloud and HBM4 with Samsung — signals an accelerating effort to erect a credible alternative to Nvidia's ecosystem in Asian markets. Combined with Steve Cohen's concentrated positioning in Arista and TSMC, the institutional read is that the AI infrastructure trade is broadening from a single-stock Nvidia thesis to a network and foundry layer thesis. Investors should monitor any additional partnership or capacity announcements from AMD, Samsung, or SK Hynix as the week closes.

OpenAI leadership instability and capital allocation concerns are worth tracking as a secondary risk to the broader AI sentiment narrative. Fidji Simo's medical leave, coming alongside the TBPN acquisition critique and the lingering questions about the Jony Ive hardware bet, introduces a governance and execution uncertainty that the market has largely ignored given OpenAI's product dominance. If any of these stories develops further today — particularly around product roadmap delays or organizational conflict — it could weigh on AI-adjacent names that have priced in OpenAI partnership optionality.
