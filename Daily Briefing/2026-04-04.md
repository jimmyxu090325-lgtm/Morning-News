1. US TECH AND AI

The dominant story in US tech this week is the accelerating convergence of AI infrastructure, space, and geopolitical risk, creating a volatile but opportunity-rich backdrop for sophisticated investors. The AMD-versus-Nvidia debate has resurfaced with renewed force, with analysts arguing that the AI supercycle is large enough to sustain both chipmakers at elevated valuations. Nvidia retains its commanding position in AI accelerator shipments, but AMD's deepening Asian partnerships — specifically a sovereign AI infrastructure agreement with NAVER Cloud and an expanded HBM4 supply collaboration with Samsung — signal that AMD is systematically closing the gap in the ecosystem dimension, not just the silicon dimension. For investors, this means the competitive moat is narrowing at the margin, though Nvidia's CUDA software lock-in and dominant HBM supply relationships with SK Hynix remain formidable barriers. Watch for any guidance revisions from either company as HBM supply constraints evolve.

On the memory side, the HBM bottleneck remains structurally intact despite Google's TurboQuant quantization breakthrough, which reduces memory bandwidth pressure at the model inference layer but does not eliminate demand at the training layer. This is a meaningful distinction. Micron's positioning in HBM3E and its anticipated ramp into HBM4 remains a significant catalyst, and the supply-demand imbalance should keep average selling prices elevated through at least the second half of 2026. Taiwan Semiconductor continues to anchor the entire supply chain, and Steve Cohen's Point72 has been building a position in TSM, lending institutional credibility to the long thesis at a moment when export control risks remain the central overhang.

The SpaceX-xAI merger, now carrying a potential IPO valuation north of two trillion dollars, is the single largest narrative reshaping how investors think about the intersection of AI and infrastructure. Bloomberg's reporting that SpaceX is targeting a June listing at up to 75 billion dollars in proceeds would make it the largest IPO in history by a considerable margin. The merged entity — combining Starlink's global connectivity infrastructure with xAI's Grok model ecosystem — creates a vertically integrated AI-to-edge platform that has no direct comparable. For listed proxies, this benefits companies with satellite adjacency, which explains the outsized moves in Planet Labs, Iridium, and Intuitive Machines this week. Amazon's concurrent talks to acquire Globalstar for Project Kuiper expansion adds a second hyperscaler to the low-earth-orbit connectivity race, raising the strategic stakes considerably.

Apple is a quieter story today, though Warren Buffett's public admission that he sold Apple too soon — and would buy more in a different market environment — is a meaningful signal about the stock's long-term earnings quality even as near-term macro conditions give him pause. Berkshire retains Apple as its largest holding, which provides a valuation floor of sorts given the scale of that institutional conviction. OpenAI's acquisition of TBPN, a media company, alongside the medical leave of product chief Fidji Simo, introduces operational uncertainty at a moment when OpenAI is navigating an increasingly complex competitive and regulatory landscape. Greg Brockman stepping into product oversight is a stabilizing factor, but investors in OpenAI's ecosystem stocks should monitor whether this leadership turbulence affects partnership cadences with Microsoft.

The Amplify QDVO product, offering synthetic income from the Magnificent Seven basket at 10.7%, illustrates a broader market dynamic worth noting. When structured income products built on growth stocks attract meaningful inflows, it often signals that the marginal retail investor is anchoring on volatility premiums rather than capital appreciation, which can be an early indicator of positioning saturation in mega-cap tech. This does not constitute a sell signal on its own, but it is worth weighting in the context of the market valuation warning below.

The broader market valuation concern flagged this week is serious. With US equities pricing in a sustained AI earnings ramp while simultaneously absorbing geopolitical risk premiums from the Iran conflict and tariff overhang, the risk-reward in broad indices is asymmetric to the downside. Sophisticated investors should be thinking about concentration risk within their AI-exposed positions.

2. GLOBAL MACRO
----------------------------------------------------------------------

The macro picture this morning is decisively risk-off, driven by three simultaneous shocks: a hotter-than-expected payrolls print, an active military conflict involving US forces in Iran, and commodity price surges in defense-critical materials. The combination is stagflationary in character and is precisely the environment that punishes duration-heavy assets and forces a reassessment of the Fed's forward path.

The March payrolls report printed at 178,000, dramatically exceeding the consensus expectation of 59,000. The unemployment rate rose modestly to 4.3 percent from 4.4 percent, which at face value looks like a softening in labor conditions, but the payrolls beat is the more market-relevant number and it materially reduces the probability of a May Fed rate cut. Markets had been pricing in two to three cuts in 2026; this data point pushes the first cut firmly into the second half of the year at the earliest, and if the Iran conflict generates sustained energy price inflation, even that timeline becomes questionable. The US Treasury market's growing anxiety about inflation, noted in multiple sources this morning, is being expressed through yield curve steepening, with the 10-year under particular pressure.

The geopolitical dimension is not a tail risk this morning — it is the central risk. A US fighter jet has been shot down or downed in Iran, one crew member has been rescued, and President Trump has publicly threatened to destroy Iranian infrastructure, having already announced sustained military strikes on Tehran over the next two to three weeks. This is an active military confrontation between the United States and Iran, and the implications cascade across every asset class. Oil should be trading significantly higher on Strait of Hormuz risk, gold should be bid as a safe-haven, and defense sector equities should outperform. Copper and other industrial metals tied to defense electronics supply chains — particularly those where China controls upstream supply — are also surging, as reported this morning. This matters directly for the semiconductor sector because several of the critical rare earth and specialty elements used in advanced chip packaging and defense electronics are produced or processed predominantly in China.

Warren Buffett's public statement that an Iranian bomb would make nuclear disaster harder to avoid is not merely a philosophical observation from a 95-year-old statesman of capital. It is a material risk assessment from the world's most closely watched allocator, and it should be interpreted as a signal that geopolitical tail risks are being priced into Berkshire's decision-making framework. His concurrent note about making only a tiny new investment in this environment reinforces the message: this is a moment for capital preservation, not aggressive deployment.

The dollar's behavior in this environment will be complex. On one hand, the strong payrolls print is dollar-positive via rate differentials. On the other hand, Iran conflict risk and the one-year anniversary of Liberation Day tariffs serve as reminders that the structural dollar demand from trade partners has been eroding. The DXY is likely to see elevated volatility rather than a clean directional move. Commodity traders should watch Brent crude for any break above the 95-dollar level as a signal that the Iran risk premium is being fully incorporated. Gold at or above recent highs is consistent with the combination of real yield pressure and geopolitical demand.

The one-year Liberation Day tariff anniversary is worth reflecting on analytically. As CNBC reports, US industries including retail and autos have spent twelve months restructuring their supply chains, risk models, and pricing strategies around the new trade architecture. The lingering effects are disinflationary in some categories where onshoring has improved supply predictability, but inflationary in others where substitution has proven impossible. The net effect is a K-shaped inflation profile that the Fed finds particularly difficult to manage with a single policy rate instrument.

3. A-SHARE AND CHINA MARKETS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

China market sentiment this morning is navigating a complex intersection of domestic AI enthusiasm, EV competitive dynamics, and the indirect effects of the Iran conflict on commodity and defense-adjacent supply chains. The macro backdrop from the US — a stronger dollar, rising US yields, and heightened geopolitical risk — historically creates capital outflow pressure on emerging market assets including A-shares, but China's partial insulation from global capital flows via the managed exchange rate and domestic liquidity conditions provides a buffer that pure EM frameworks underestimate.

On the AI front, Tencent's expansion of its OpenClaw enterprise tool suite reflects the maturation of China's domestic AI agent ecosystem from experimental deployment to enterprise commercialization. The fact that Tencent is capitalizing on what the SCMP describes as the lobster craze — a reference to agentic AI workflows gaining rapid enterprise adoption — suggests that demand-side pull in China's AI sector is strengthening even as Huawei's cloud revenue disappointed in 2025. Huawei's cloud underperformance relative to US hyperscalers, even when including internal customers in the revenue count, is a structural signal about the quality of China's AI cloud stack. This creates a nuanced picture: model-layer and agent-layer companies in China are gaining traction, but the infrastructure layer remains a competitive vulnerability relative to AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud.

Hong Kong is actively positioning itself as the internationalization launchpad for Chinese enterprises, with the 2026 SCMP Global Connections conference highlighting the pivot from traditional export models to full global ecosystem buildout. This is strategically relevant for investors in Hong Kong-listed Chinese conglomerates with overseas ambitions. New World Development's decision to shelve Causeway Bay commercial property acquisitions is a cautionary note about the unevenness of Hong Kong's real estate recovery — demand signals are improving in prime office but not yet strong enough to justify speculative accumulation by leveraged developers.

Tesla's recapture of the global pure EV sales crown from BYD in Q1 2026, with a 6.5 percent year-on-year delivery increase, is a nuanced data point for A-share EV investors. BYD's strength in hybrid and plug-in hybrid vehicles remains uncontested, and its domestic market position is structurally secure, but the narrative that Chinese EV makers had permanently overtaken Tesla in pure electric volume has been interrupted. For Leapmotor, which is accelerating its European R&D presence and exploring Canadian assembly, the path to global scale is becoming more complex as Western markets impose additional regulatory and tariff barriers on Chinese vehicle imports.

The commodity angle is the most actionable China theme for today. Three niche commodity prices are surging in the wake of the Iran conflict, and while the specific elements are not named in the news feed, the reference to critical materials used in defense electronics and AI semiconductors points squarely at gallium, germanium, and rare earth processing — all domains where China's export controls from late 2024 continue to bite. For A-share investors, this means the defense materials and specialty chemicals sectors deserve elevated attention, as geopolitical risk translates into pricing power for Chinese upstream producers in these categories. The CSI 300 as a whole will likely face pressure from risk-off contagion from US markets, but sector rotation into defense, materials, and domestic AI infrastructure should partially offset index-level weakness.

The MSCI index review completed in February 2026 may continue to influence foreign institutional investor flows into select A-share names. Any stocks that received weighting increases in that review will have a structural bid from passive trackers, which is worth cross-referencing against the focus stocks below.

世纪华通 / Century Huatong (002602):
Century Huatong operates primarily in mobile games and has been navigating a restructuring of its content portfolio following regulatory normalization in China's gaming sector. The stock has moderate sensitivity to domestic consumer discretionary sentiment. This week's global risk-off environment from the Iran conflict and potential A-share outflows creates near-term headwind. Watch for any announcements on new game title approvals from the National Press and Publication Administration, which remain the key catalyst for re-rating. The company's exposure to mobile internet advertising as a secondary revenue stream also ties it loosely to broader digital ad market conditions, where Tencent's enterprise AI push could indirectly benefit distribution ecosystems.

中信金属 / CITIC Metal (601061):
CITIC Metal is directly in the crosshairs of this morning's most important commodity theme. As a major metals trading and investment platform under the CITIC Group umbrella, it has exposure to the specialty and strategic metals categories that are surging on Iran conflict-driven supply chain anxiety. The three niche commodity prices flagged in the news feed — likely including gallium, germanium, and related defense-critical elements — overlap with CITIC Metal's portfolio of minority stakes in upstream mining assets. This is a potential outperformer today despite broad market weakness. Watch volume and the behavior of the broader nonferrous metals sector on the Shanghai exchange as a real-time read on whether institutional investors are rotating into this theme.

五矿新能 / Minmetals New Energy (002628):
Minmetals New Energy sits at the intersection of state-owned resource capital and the new energy transition supply chain. Its exposure to lithium, cobalt, and related battery materials gives it a dual sensitivity to EV demand signals — which are complicated by Tesla's Q1 recapture of the EV crown — and to geopolitical commodity pricing, which is constructive this morning. The Iran conflict's effect on energy markets creates a secondary tailwind for new energy as a structural alternative to hydrocarbon dependency, which Beijing consistently uses as a policy narrative to support domestic new energy champions. Monitor PBOC liquidity signals and any NDRC statements on new energy industrial policy for directional cues.

恒邦股份 / Hengbang Co. (002237):
Hengbang is a gold and copper smelting company, which makes it one of the more directly actionable names in today's environment. Gold is in strong safe-haven demand given the Iran military confrontation and geopolitical uncertainty flagged by Buffett. Copper faces a more complex picture — industrial demand is moderated by global growth concerns, but defense electronics and AI data center construction provide structural support. Hengbang's smelting margins are sensitive to the spread between input ore prices and refined metal prices, so watch LME copper and Shanghai gold futures closely. If gold holds above recent resistance levels and copper remains firm despite risk-off pressure, Hengbang is a tactical long candidate within the A-share materials complex.

雅克科技 / Yake Technology (002409):
Yake Technology is a specialty chemicals company with significant exposure to semiconductor materials, including precursors and process chemicals used in advanced chip fabrication. This makes it a direct beneficiary of both the domestic semiconductor self-sufficiency push and the global HBM capacity expansion theme. The HBM bottleneck analysis in today's news feed — noting that demand pressure persists despite Google's TurboQuant advance — is constructive for Yake's order book. AMD's expanded HBM4 collaboration with Samsung also implies continued strong demand for the specialty chemicals used in next-generation memory fabrication. Watch for any supply chain commentary from TSMC or Samsung that might validate domestic Chinese wafer fab expansion timelines, which would be the primary volume driver for Yake.

三峡能源 / Three Gorges Energy (600905):
Three Gorges Energy is one of China's largest clean energy operators, with a portfolio weighted toward offshore wind and utility-scale solar. The stock's investment thesis is built around China's renewables capacity expansion targets and the regulatory tariff structures that determine project returns. In today's macro environment, the Iran conflict creates upward pressure on thermal energy costs, which historically improves the relative economics of renewable power projects and can accelerate policy support timelines. The company's SOE status provides a degree of insulation from private capital market volatility. Watch for any NDRC or National Energy Administration announcements on offshore wind concession rounds or feed-in tariff structures, which are the primary near-term catalysts.

中国电建 / PowerChina (601669):
PowerChina is the engineering, procurement, and construction giant behind much of China's hydropower and renewable energy infrastructure, as well as significant overseas project work across Belt and Road Initiative countries. The Iran conflict has a direct relevance to PowerChina's overseas project exposure — while PowerChina's Middle East presence is primarily in Gulf Cooperation Council countries rather than Iran itself, regional instability creates operational risk across a geography where the company has meaningful contract backlog. Domestically, PowerChina benefits from the same energy transition investment cycle as Three Gorges Energy. Watch for contract announcement flows from the company's investor relations channels, and monitor whether overseas project risk is being re-priced by the market given the Iran escalation.

This bifurcation has direct implications for sector allocation within A-shares — favor application-layer AI plays and specialty materials over pure cloud infrastructure bets until evidence of competitive improvement in the infrastructure layer emerges.
